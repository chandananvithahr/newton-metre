## 1. Defense Manufacturing (India)

### 1.1 Industry Context

India's defense production reached INR 1,26,887 crore (USD ~15.2B) in FY2024, up from INR 1,06,826 crore in FY2023. The government targets USD 25B by 2025 under "Make in India" defense policy. Key public sector units include HAL, BEL, BDL, BEML, Munitions India (ex-OFB), and the newly corporatized AWEIL (Armoured Vehicles Nigam Ltd). Private sector players include L&T Defence, Tata Advanced Systems (TASL), Bharat Forge (Kalyani Group), Godrej Aerospace, and VEM Technologies.

| 5 | **Sand Casting** | 6-8% | Large vehicle hulls, turret bases, engine blocks, pump housings | BEML, HAL F&F, ordnance factories |

## 2. Aerospace Manufacturing (India)

### 2.1 Industry Context

Indian aerospace manufacturing is anchored by HAL (revenue INR 30,381 crore in FY2025, order book INR 1.8 lakh crore). The private sector is growing rapidly: Tata Boeing (Apache fuselages), Mahindra Aerostructures, Dynamatic Technologies (Airbus Beluga cargo door ribs), Aequs (machined aerospace components), and Godrej Aerospace (rocket engines, satellite structures). India's aerostructure exports reached ~USD 1.5B in FY2024.

### 2.2 TOP 10 Processes for Indian Aerospace

| Rank | Process | Est. % of Parts | Key Part Types | Primary Players |
|------|---------|----------------|----------------|----------------|

| 2 | **CNC Turning** | 12-18% | Engine shafts, landing gear pins, fasteners, actuator bodies, fittings | HAL, Aequs, Godrej |
| 3 | **Sheet Metal Forming (Stretch + Hydroform + Brake)** | 10-15% | Fuselage skins, wing panels, fairings, ducts, brackets | HAL, TASL, Mahindra Aero |

| 5 | **Investment Casting** | 6-8% | Turbine blades, vanes, structural castings, fittings | HAL F&F, Uni Deritend, Precision Castparts |
| 6 | **Closed Die Forging** | 5-8% | Landing gear, engine discs, fan blades, structural fittings | HAL F&F, Bharat Forge |

| **Additive manufacturing** | 1-2% (growing — GE LEAP has 19 AM fuel nozzles/engine) |

### 2.4 5-Axis Milling vs Turning vs Sheet Metal vs Composite

For structural aerostructure work (the bulk of Indian aerospace contract manufacturing):

| Process | % of Structural Parts | Trend |
|---------|----------------------|-------|
| 5-axis milling (from billet/forging) | 35-45% | Stable — monolithic parts reduce assembly |
| Sheet metal (formed skins/panels) | 20-25% | Declining as composites grow |
| Composites | 15-20% (new platforms: 30-50%) | Rapidly growing |
| Turned components | 10-15% | Stable |
| Cast + forged (near-net then machined) | 10-15% | Stable |

**Key insight:** On new-generation aircraft (LCA Mk2, AMCA), composite content targets 40-50% of airframe weight, but by part count, machined metal parts still dominate due to hundreds of brackets, fittings, and connectors per aircraft.

## 3. Automobile Manufacturing (India)

### 3.1 Industry Context

India's auto component industry turnover reached INR 6,73,000 crore (USD ~78.7B) in FY2025, with a CAGR of 14% (FY20-FY25). India is the world's 2nd largest casting producer (~15.86 million MT in FY2025, USD 19B turnover) and 2nd largest forging producer (installed capacity 38.5 lakh MT, USD 5.35B market in 2024). The automotive sector consumes approximately 40-50% of all castings and 58% of all forgings produced in India.

Key players: Bharat Forge, Sundaram Fasteners, Motherson Group, Minda Industries, Endurance Technologies, Bosch India, ZF India, Sona BLW.

### 4.2 The Critical 20% — 15 Processes That Cover 80%+ of All Parts

Based on the matrix above, the following **15 processes** account for approximately **80-85% of all manufactured parts** across defense, aerospace, and automobile sectors:

**Tier 1 — The Big 6 (covers ~60-65% of all parts):**

| Priority | Process | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| P0 | **CNC Turning** | Most common single machining operation. Every sector. Shafts, barrels, fasteners, pins. |
| P0 | **CNC Milling (3-axis)** | Flat surfaces, pockets, slots. Post-cast/post-forge finish machining. Universal. |
| P0 | **CNC Milling (5-axis)** | Dominant in defense + aerospace. Monolithic structural parts. Highest value. |
| P0 | **Sheet Metal (Laser Cut + Press Brake Bend)** | Enclosures, brackets, panels. High volume in defense electronics + aerospace skins. |
| P0 | **Closed Die Forging** | Crankshafts, gears, landing gear, gun parts. High-value, high-strength. |
| P0 | **Die Casting (HPDC)** | Engine blocks, housings, structural nodes. Dominates auto. Growing in EV. |

**Tier 2 — The Next 5 (covers ~15-20% more):**

| Priority | Process | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| P1 | **Stamping / Press Work** | Body panels, brackets, hardware. Massive volume in auto. |
| P1 | **Sand Casting** | Large parts: engine blocks, vehicle hulls, pump housings. Legacy but still huge. |
| P1 | **Investment Casting** | Turbine blades, missile fins, precision parts. Critical for aero/defense. |
| P1 | **Drilling** | Present in nearly every machined part. Sub-process of milling/turning. |
| P1 | **Grinding (Cylindrical + Surface)** | Finish process for precision fits. Always follows turning/milling. |

**Tier 3 — Sector-Specific Completers (covers ~5-10% more):**

| Priority | Process | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------|---------------|
| P2 | **Injection Molding (Plastic)** | Huge in auto (8-10%). Not metal, but critical for auto cost estimation. |
| P2 | **Gear Cutting (Hobbing + Shaving)** | Transmission gears. Specialized but high-value. |
| P2 | **Composite Layup + Autoclave** | Growing in aerospace. Critical for next-gen aircraft. |
| P2 | **Welding (TIG + MIG + Spot)** | Assembly process. Present in all sectors. |

## 6. Data Requirements Per Process

### 6.1 What We Need to Estimate Cost for Each Priority Process

**CNC Turning (HAVE — Phase 1):**
- Material type, diameter, length
- Operations: OD turning, ID boring, facing, threading
- Cutting parameters: speed, feed, depth of cut (from Sandvik data)
- Machine rate ₹/hr, setup time, tool cost

**CNC Milling (HAVE — Phase 1):**
- Material type, dimensions (L x W x H)
- Operations: face, slot, pocket, contour, drilling
- MRR-based time calculation (from cutting data)
- Machine rate ₹/hr, setup time, tool cost

**Sheet Metal — Laser Cutting (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Material type, thickness
- Cut perimeter (mm) — from drawing
- Laser cut speed table: material x thickness → mm/min
- Machine rate ₹/hr (fiber laser: ₹1500-2500/hr, CO2: ₹800-1200/hr)
- Sheet utilization / nesting efficiency (typically 70-85%)
- Gas cost (N2 for stainless, O2 for mild steel)

**Sheet Metal — Press Brake Bending (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Number of bends, bend length
- Material type, thickness
- V-die opening selection (8x thickness rule)
- Tonnage required = (material tensile x thickness^2 x bend length) / (V-die opening x 1.33)
- Machine rate ₹/hr (₹600-1000/hr)
- Setup time per bend (5-15 min first bend, 2-5 min subsequent)

**Closed Die Forging (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Part weight (finished)
- Material type (steel, Al, Ti)
- Forging ratio (billet weight / part weight) — typically 1.3-2.0x
- Material cost ₹/kg
- Die cost (₹50K-₹20L depending on complexity) amortized over lot size
- Press tonnage → machine rate ₹/hr
- Number of forging operations (preform + finish)
- Post-forge machining allowance (typically 2-5mm per side)

**Die Casting — HPDC (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Part weight (net)
- Material: Al alloy (A380, A356), Zn, Mg
- Shot weight = part + runner + overflow (typically 1.3-1.8x part weight)
- Cycle time: lock → inject → cool → eject (15-90 sec depending on size)
- Machine tonnage = projected area x cavity pressure (700-1000 kg/cm2)
- Machine rate ₹/hr by tonnage (400T: ₹1200/hr, 800T: ₹2000/hr, 1600T: ₹3500/hr)
- Die cost (₹5L-₹50L) amortized over die life (80K-300K shots)
- Trim + deburr + X-ray (if structural)

**Sand Casting (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Part weight, material (CI, steel, Al, bronze)
- Casting yield: net weight / gross weight (typically 50-70%)
- Pattern cost amortized (wood: ₹10K-₹50K, metal: ₹50K-₹3L)
- Moulding cost: ₹/kg or ₹/mould
- Melting cost: ₹/kg (material + energy)
- Fettling (grinding, cutting gates/risers): ₹/kg
- Heat treatment if required
- Machining allowance: 3-8mm per side

**Investment Casting (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Part weight, material (Ni superalloy, Ti, SS, Al)
- Wax cost: ₹/kg wax x pattern weight
- Shell cost: ₹/layer x number of layers (5-8 layers typical)
- Cluster size: parts per tree (5-50 depending on size)
- Metal yield: 40-60%
- Machine rate for shelling, dewaxing, pouring, knockout
- Post-cast: HIP, heat treatment, machining, NDT

**Stamping (NEED — Phase 2):**
- Material type, thickness, blank size
- Number of stations (progressive die: 4-12 stations)
- Press tonnage required
- Die cost (₹5L-₹50L for progressive) amortized over volume
- Stroke rate: 20-60 strokes/min (production), 5-10 (prototype)
- Material utilization: 60-85% (depends on blank layout)
- Secondary operations: deburring, tapping, welding

## Summary: What to Build Next

**Current state (Phase 1):** Costimize covers 18 machining + finishing processes = ~40-45% of addressable parts.

**Biggest gap:** Sheet metal (laser + bend) + casting (die + sand + investment) + forging = ~35% of parts NOT covered.

**Recommendation:** Phase 2 priority order:
1. **Sheet metal (laser cut + bend)** — Fastest to implement, high volume in defense/aero, data is straightforward (cut length x speed table)
2. **Closed die forging** — Covers auto + defense, high value, data is weight-based
3. **Die casting (HPDC)** — Dominates auto, cycle-time-based model
4. **Sand casting** — Large legacy base, weight-based model
5. **Investment casting** — Critical for aero, complex but well-documented model
6. **Stamping** — High volume auto, tonnage-based model

After Phase 2, Costimize will cover ~80% of all parts quoted by Indian MSME job shops and Tier-2/3 suppliers across defense, aerospace, and automobile sectors.
