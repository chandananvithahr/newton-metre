# How YC Startups Got Their First Customers

> Research compiled March 2026. Real stories, specific tactics, actual numbers.

---

## 1. The Core Philosophy: "Do Things That Don't Scale"

Paul Graham's 2013 essay is the foundational text. Key principles:

- **Startups take off because founders make them take off.** There is no magic growth fairy. You are the engine.
- **Recruit users manually.** The most common unscalable thing founders do at the start.
- **"Collison Installation":** When anyone agreed to try Stripe, Patrick and John Collison would say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot. Don't send a link. Install it yourself.
- **Keep the fire contained.** Facebook was only at Harvard until it had critical mass, then spread to other Ivy League schools. Don't try to boil the ocean.
- **Avoid big launches.** A big buzzy launch with embargoes and press articles is often a mistake.
- **Create an insanely great experience for your first few users.** Over-serve them.

## 2. Pre-YC Traction Numbers (What It Actually Takes)

### What YC accepts:
- **~40% of each batch is just an idea** with no product, no revenue
- **Most of the batch is pre-revenue** at time of acceptance
- **7% had >$50K MRR** when accepted (the outliers)
- Some companies like Rappi and MessageBird did YC with >$1M/year revenue
- **~30% of a batch** will have zero revenue even at Demo Day

### What counts as "traction" without revenue:
- Users (even a handful)
- Waitlist signups
- Letters of Intent (LOIs) -- especially for hardware/capital-intensive startups
- Deep customer interviews (showing you understand the problem)
- A working prototype with any usage at all

### The takeaway for Costimize:
You do NOT need revenue to get into YC. But having 3-5 paying customers or LOIs from procurement teams would put you in the top half of the batch. Even 1 real customer using your tool on a real part negotiation is strong signal.

## 3. Specific Company Stories

### Stripe (YC S2009)
- **First customer:** Ross Boucher at 280 North (a YC company). Within 2 weeks of building the prototype, they had first transactions.
- **Tactic:** The "Collison Installation" -- physically taking people's laptops and setting up Stripe on the spot. No "we'll send you a link" -- immediate installation.
- **First 10-30 customers:** All from Y Combinator network.
- **Growth strategy for a long time:** Write good blog posts, build a good product, hope people tell others. That's it.
- **Key lesson:** Your first customers are people who already trust you. YC batchmates were the captive audience.

### Airbnb (YC W2009)
- **Pre-YC traction:** ~50 people visiting the site daily, 10-20 bookings/day after 1.5 years of work.
- **First hosts:** The founders themselves -- hosted 3 strangers in their San Francisco apartment (from Boston, Utah, India). Slept under the same roof as their first users.
- **Craigslist hack:** Built software to extract contact info of Craigslist property listers and recruit them to list on Airbnb. Also created cross-posting to Craigslist.
- **Professional photography:** Flew to New York, went door-to-door to hosts, photographed apartments themselves (couldn't afford real photographers initially).
- **MVP was barebones:** No payments, no map view, built by a part-time developer.
- **Survival tactic:** Made and sold their own cereal (Obama O's, Cap'n McCain's) during 2008 election to fund operations -- earned $30K.

### DoorDash (YC S2013)
- **Origin:** Stanford students. Co-founder sat down with a macaroon store owner in Palo Alto who showed him a booklet of delivery orders she had to turn down because she couldn't fulfill them.
- **First experiment:** Spent ONE AFTERNOON making a landing page called "Palo Alto Delivery" with PDF menus of local restaurants and their personal cell phone numbers. "We just launched it and all we wanted to see was would we get phone calls."
- **First customer:** Got a call the same day. Thai food order. Founders delivered it themselves.
- **Growth:** Day 1: 1 call. Day 2: 2 calls. Day 3: 5. Then 7. Then 10.
- **Tactic:** Walked down University Avenue in Palo Alto handing out flyers.
- **Operations:** Founders were the delivery drivers AND customer support. "I sometimes had to take phone calls during lectures."
- **Key lesson:** They would never have come up with this idea in a conference room. They needed to be with customers trying things out.

### Brex (YC W2017)
- **Pivot story:** Entered YC with a VR startup idea. Noticed that startups (including themselves) couldn't get corporate credit cards from traditional banks. Pivoted to fintech.
- **First customers:** YC batchmates. Being part of YC was the built-in distribution channel.
- **Speed advantage:** Delivered first cards in 4 months. Customers approved in 24 hours with no paperwork (vs. weeks at traditional banks).
- **Value proposition:** 10-20x higher credit limits than American Express by underwriting based on startup capital raised.
- **Traction:** 85 customers by July 2017 (a few months post-launch). Then 85 to 2,000+ within 3 months of Series B.

### Vanta (YC W2018)
- **First 10 customers:** Went through the list of old YC companies. Worked with their YC partner to prioritize targets. Wrote outbound emails to founders.
- **Tactic:** Searched YC's internal forum (Bookface) and emailed anyone who had ever mentioned the word "compliance" in the prior decade.
- **Quote:** "This is why we did YC" -- specifically for customer access.

### Gusto (YC W2012)
- **First 10 customers:** Friends who were starting businesses in California -- mostly YC batch startups, plus non-tech small businesses.
- **Non-obvious customer:** A children's swimming camp. The swimming instructor heard about them from her brother, who was in a startup they knew.
- **Key lesson:** First customers come from trust circles, not cold outreach.

### Zapier (YC S2012)
- **First 8 months:** Founder Wade spent hours searching through forums of other SaaS products, finding people complaining about missing integrations. He'd respond, offer to help, build the integration, and convert them.
- **Key lesson:** Go where your users are already complaining about the problem you solve.

### Retool (YC W2017)
- **Tactic:** Bought a Crunchbase account. Filtered companies by last fundraise date and vertical. Avoided SaaS companies. Targeted delivery startups and fintech companies. Cold emailed CTOs and VPs of operations.
- **Key lesson:** Cold outbound works when you're surgical about targeting.

### Figma
- **First users:** Talked to practically every designer they knew. Used Twitter to identify the most influential designers. Cold emailed or found introductions to those people and showed them Figma.
- **Key lesson:** Identify the influencers in your target user base and get them using your product.

### Segment
- **Early growth:** Got engineers trying the product on side projects, who then told them what they'd need to bring it into their companies. Levered their way up into bigger markets.
- **First non-friend user:** Jon Ma (Public Comps). 20-minute conversation, API key handoff, production traffic 8 hours later.

### Wufoo
- **Tactic:** Sent a hand-written thank-you card in the mail to every single new user who signed up for their web app. Physical card. In the mail.

## 4. Patterns and Playbook

### The Trust Circles Model (Lenny Rachitsky)

Your first B2B customers come from concentric circles of decreasing trust:

```
Circle 1: Friends & family who fit ICP          (highest trust)
Circle 2: Former colleagues / classmates
Circle 3: Friends of friends / warm intros
Circle 4: YC batch / accelerator network
Circle 5: Investor network introductions
Circle 6: Community engagement (forums, events)
Circle 7: Content marketing / online presence
Circle 8: Cold outbound                         (lowest trust)
```

Most founders find their first customers in Circles 1-3.

### The 7 Tactics That Actually Work Pre-PMF

| # | Tactic | Example | Effort |
|---|--------|---------|--------|
| 1 | **Personal network** | Gusto's friends starting businesses | Low |
| 2 | **Collison Installation** | Set it up on their laptop right now | Medium |
| 3 | **Go where users complain** | Zapier in SaaS forums | Medium |
| 4 | **Surgical cold outbound** | Retool bought Crunchbase, filtered by vertical | Medium |
| 5 | **Door-to-door / in-person** | DoorDash flyers, Airbnb photography | High |
| 6 | **Free work / consulting** | Build anything for anybody (Identity.com) | High |
| 7 | **Community/forum presence** | Dropbox's Digg video, Figma's designer Twitter | Medium |

### What Nobody Did:
- Paid ads (too early, no conversion data)
- PR / press launches (Paul Graham explicitly warns against this)
- Building features nobody asked for
- Waiting for inbound

### B2B-Specific Patterns:
1. **Hand-to-hand combat is mandatory.** No shortcut at the beginning.
2. **Your first customer will feel like consulting.** That's OK. Learn what they need.
3. **Avoid becoming a consultancy.** Build for the pattern, not for one customer's edge case.
4. **LOIs are valid traction.** If you can't get payment, get a signed letter of intent.
5. **Speed of onboarding wins.** Brex: 24hrs vs. banks' weeks. Stripe: 7 lines of code vs. weeks of integration.

## 5. What This Means for Costimize / AI.Procurve

### Your ICP overlap opportunities:

**Circle 1-2 (Trust-based):**
- Your 7 years in defense manufacturing = you know procurement people personally
- Former colleagues, suppliers, buyers you've worked with
- People who would take your call because they know you

**Circle 3-4 (Warm intros):**
- Defense/aerospace industry contacts who can intro you to their procurement teams
- LinkedIn connections in Indian manufacturing

**Specific tactics to try NOW:**

1. **The DoorDash test:** Make a simple landing page. "Upload your drawing, get a should-cost breakdown." Put your WhatsApp/phone number on it. Share it in 3 manufacturing WhatsApp groups. See if anyone calls.

2. **The Collison Installation:** When someone shows interest, don't say "I'll send you the link." Say "Send me a drawing right now, I'll run it through and show you the breakdown on a call." Do it live.

3. **The Zapier forum tactic:** Search LinkedIn, IndiaMart forums, defense procurement forums for people complaining about supplier pricing, cost estimation, or negotiation. Respond with value.

4. **The Vanta tactic (for YC application):** If you apply to YC, having even 3 procurement teams who have used your tool on a real part negotiation is top-decile traction for an idea-stage company.

5. **The consulting bridge:** Offer to do 5 free should-cost analyses for procurement teams. Use your tool. Show them the line-by-line breakdown. Then ask: "Would you pay for this if it was self-serve?"

6. **LOIs for YC application:** Get 3-5 signed letters of intent from procurement managers saying "I would pay $X/month for this tool." Even without revenue, this is valid traction.

### The number to aim for:
- **Pre-YC application:** 3-10 users who have actually used the tool on real parts
- **At YC acceptance:** Evidence that procurement teams find the should-cost breakdown valuable enough to change their negotiation behavior
- **By Demo Day:** 10-50 paying customers or strong LOI pipeline
