## 1. Break-Even Analysis & Indian Manufacturing Costs (Ch 1, p3-8)

### 1.1 Break-Even Formula

Break-even quantity between two processes:

```
Q_be = (F2 - F1) / (V1 - V2)
```

Where:
- F1, F2 = Fixed costs (tooling, equipment) for processes 1 and 2
- V1, V2 = Variable cost per unit for processes 1 and 2

### 2.2 Mechanical Properties of Engineering Materials (p12-14)

**Key property definitions with typical values:**
- **Proportional limit:** Stress up to which stress is proportional to strain (Hooke's law)
- **Elastic limit:** Max stress without permanent deformation
- **Yield strength (0.2% offset):** Stress at 0.2% permanent strain
- **Ultimate tensile strength (UTS):** Maximum stress before necking
- **Modulus of elasticity (E):** Slope of stress-strain curve in elastic region
- **Resilience:** Energy absorbed per unit volume in elastic range = sigma_y^2 / (2E)
- **Toughness:** Total energy absorbed to fracture (area under full stress-strain curve)

### 2.4 Hardness Test Parameters (p11-13)

| Test | Indenter | Load | Application |
|------|----------|------|-------------|
| Brinell (BHN) | 10mm steel/WC ball | 500, 1500, 3000 kgf | General purpose, soft-medium materials |
| Rockwell B (HRB) | 1/16" steel ball | 100 kgf (10 minor + 90 major) | Soft steels, brass, bronze |
| Rockwell C (HRC) | 120 deg diamond cone | 150 kgf (10 minor + 140 major) | Hard steels, heat-treated |
| Vickers (VHN) | 136 deg diamond pyramid | 1-120 kgf | All materials, most accurate |
| Knoop (HK) | Elongated diamond pyramid | Low | Thin sections, coatings |
| Shore (Scleroscope) | Diamond-tipped hammer | Drop from 254mm | Quick shop-floor test |

**BHN formula:** BHN = 2P / (pi * D * (D - sqrt(D^2 - d^2)))
- P = applied load (kgf), D = ball diameter (mm), d = impression diameter (mm)

**BHN-UTS approximate relationships:**
- For steel: UTS (MPa) = 3.45 x BHN
- For cast iron: UTS (MPa) = varies non-linearly

### 2.5 Steel Classification (p24)

**Three classes of plain carbon steels:**

| Class | Carbon % | Properties |
|-------|----------|------------|
| Low carbon | up to 0.30% | Soft, ductile, good weldability, not heat-treatable (except carburizing) |
| Medium carbon | 0.30 to 0.60% | Better machinability, higher tensile strength, responsive to heat treatment |
| High carbon | 0.60 to 1.70% | Highest strength, hardest, used for cutting tools, springs, dies |

**Effects of carbon on properties:**
- Tensile strength: increases with carbon content
- Hardness: increases with carbon content
- Ductility: decreases with carbon content
- Weldability: decreases with carbon content

Where element symbols represent weight percentages.

### 2.8 Indian Standard (IS) Steel Designations (p30-32)

**IS Designation System:**
- Plain carbon steels (no heat treatment): designated by tensile strength in kgf/mm2
  - e.g., "St 42" = structural steel with minimum UTS of 42 kgf/mm2
- Plain carbon steels (with carbon as chief alloying element): Chemical symbol C + carbon percentage in hundredths
  - e.g., "C20" = 0.20% carbon steel, "C100" = 1.00% carbon steel
  - If S and P are to be denoted as limited, suffix K: e.g., C25K
- Tool steels: Letter T instead of C
  - e.g., "T103" = tool steel with 1.03 (0.95 to 1.10) average carbon percentage

**Alloy steel designation:** Chemical symbols of significant elements followed by percentage (rounded up)
- e.g., "35Mn2Mo45" = 0.35% C, 2.00% Mn, 0.45% Mo
- e.g., "T35Cr5Mo1V30" = Tool steel per IS:3748-1966: 0.30-0.40% C, 4.75-5.25% Cr, 1.20-1.60% Mo, 0.20-0.40% V, 0.25-0.50% Mn, 0.80-1.20% Si

### 2.17 Alloying Element Effects on Steel Properties (p29-30)

| Element | Effect on Hardness | Effect on Hardenability | Other Effects |
|---------|-------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| Manganese | Moderate increase | Cheapest way to increase | Improves hot working, surface finish, counteracts S brittleness |
| Chromium | Strong increase | Increases significantly | Carbide former, wear/corrosion resistance |
| Nickel | Moderate increase | Reduces critical cooling rate | Improves toughness, elastic ratio |
| Tungsten | Strong increase | Very strong effect | Abrasion resistant, red hardness, cutting tools |
| Molybdenum | Increases | Increases greatly | Hot hardness, reduces temper embrittlement |
| Vanadium | Increases | Increases | Grain refiner, secondary hardening |
| Silicon | Moderate | Slight increase | Sustains hardness during tempering, deoxidizer |
| Aluminium | - | No effect | Most effective grain growth inhibitor, deoxidizer |
| Titanium | - | Highest carbide-forming tendency | Good deoxidizer, inhibits grain growth |
| Niobium (Columbium) | - | Decreases | Fine grain, impact strength increase |

**Critical limits for effects on carbon steel:**
- S: 0.075-0.15% for better machinability
- Mn: must be at least 3x S content
- Si: above 2.5% - poor machinability
- Mn 11-14% + C 1-1.4% = Hadfield steel (austenitic, impact/abrasion resistant)

### 3.7 Case Hardening Processes (p47-50)

**Carburizing:**
- Temperature: 800-950 deg C
- Time: 4-20 hours
- Case depth: 1-2 mm typical (up to 3.5 mm at 925 deg C for 25 hours)
- IS designation example: 15Ni1Cr65

**Cyaniding (Liquid Carbonitriding):**
- Temperature: 750-850 deg C
- Time: 30-60 minutes
- Case depth: ~0.15 mm
- Bath: sodium cyanide, potassium cyanide, or potassium ferro-cyanide

**Nitriding:**
- Temperature: 500-575 deg C
- Time: 8-40 hours (up to 125 hours in some cases)
- No quenching required
- Very hard nitrides with alloying elements (Al, Cr, V, Mo)

**Flame Hardening:**
- Case depth: up to 3 mm
- Carbon requirement: 0.3-0.6% C
- Followed by water spray quenching
- Then stress relief at ~200 deg C

**Induction Hardening:**
- Frequency: 10 kHz to 2 MHz
- Case depth: 0.25-1.5 mm
- Very fast: 5 seconds to 4 minutes
- Surface hardness: 50-60 Rc

**Laser Heat Treatment:**
- Hardness: up to 1000 HV
- Depth: up to 1.5 mm (solid-state transformation, no surface melting)
- Suitable when surface area <10% of total (otherwise conventional more economical)
- Carbon percentage: 0.3-0.6%

## 6. Gating System Design (Ch 4, p117-158)

### 6.1 Pouring Time Formulas (p131-141)

**Grey Cast Iron:**
```
t = K * (1.41 + T/14.59) * sqrt(W) seconds
```
Where:
- K = fluidity in inches / 40
- T = average section thickness in mm
- W = mass of casting in kg

**Steel:**
```
t = (2.4335 - 0.3953 * log(W)) * sqrt(W) seconds
```

### 6.2 Typical Pouring Rates for Sand Castings (Table 4.4, p131)

| Metal | Up to 10 kg (kg/s) | 10-50 kg (kg/s) | 50-100 kg (kg/s) | 100-500 kg (kg/s) |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| Cast iron | 1.1 | 1.5-2.0 | 3.0-4.0 | 3.5-6.0 |
| Steel | 1.2-1.4 | 1.9-2.5 | 2.0-5.0 | 4.5-7.0 |
| Aluminium alloys | 0.25-0.3 | 0.5-0.7 | 1.0-1.3 | 1.2-2.0 |
| Pig iron | 1.1 | 1.5-2.0 | 3.0-4.0 | 3.5-6.0 |

### 6.3 Choke Area Formula (Bernoulli's equation, p131)

```
A = W / (d * t * C * sqrt(2*g*H))
```
Where:
- A = choke area (mm2)
- W = weight of metal poured (kg) -- (actually, mass of casting / casting yield)
- d = density of liquid metal (kg/mm3)
- t = pouring time (seconds)
- C = efficiency factor (~0.73 for pressurized system with 4 in-gates)
- g = acceleration due to gravity (9810 mm/s2)
- H = effective sprue height (mm)

### 6.4 Gating Ratios (p135)

**Non-pressurized gating system:**
- Sprue : Runner : In-gate = 1 : 4 : 4
- Choke at bottom of sprue
- Used for aluminium and magnesium alloys

**Pressurized gating system:**
- In-gate is smallest cross-section
- Provides back pressure, higher casting yield
- Used for ferrous alloys

### 6.5 Casting Yield Data (p127)

```
Casting yield = (W_casting / W_total_poured) x 100%
```

Typical yields depend on:
- Casting material (higher shrinkage = lower yield)
- Shape complexity
- Size (massive/simple = higher yield)
- Typical range: 40-80%
- Assumed example value: 0.60 (60%)

### 6.6 Caine's Equation for Riser Design (p143)

**For steel castings:**
```
When height-to-diameter ratio of riser = 1:
  LM4:  X = 33.45 / (23.98 - Y)
  LM11: X = 17.11 / (12.58 - Y)

When height-to-diameter ratio of riser = 1.5:
  LM4:  X = 32.09 / (23.98 - Y)
  LM11: X = 16.75 / (12.58 - Y)
```

**For nodular iron in CO2 moulds:**
```
X = 39.44 / (27.78 - Y)
```

Where:
- X = volume of riser / volume of casting
- Y = freezing ratio (surface area to volume ratio of casting / SA to V ratio of riser)

### 6.7 Modulus Extension Factor (p155)

```
M_r = 1.2/f * M_c
```
Where:
- M_r = required riser modulus
- M_c = casting modulus
- f = modulus extension factor
- Typical f values: 1.8 for exothermic plus insulating sleeves
- Solidification time: cylindrical riser with insulating sleeve increases from 7.5 to 43 min (steel), 31.1 to 45.6 min (aluminium)

### 6.8 Casting Design Rules (p187-197)

- **Rib-to-wall thickness ratio:** 0.6 to 0.8
- **Fillet radius:** Should equal wall thickness at L-junctions; equals 0.5 inch (12.7 mm) minimum to prevent defects
- **Draft angle:** 1-3 degrees standard
- **Minimum section thickness:** Depends on metal and casting method
- **Shrinkage cavities:** Avoid isolated heavy sections; use progressive solidification toward risers

## 12. Key Insights for Cost Estimation

1. **Break-even analysis is fundamental** - Every manufacturing process choice has a crossover quantity. Our engine should recommend optimal process based on batch size.

2. **Indian foundry costs** - The book provides actual INR cost breakdowns for shell moulding vs sand casting vs die casting. These are dated but provide relative ratios.

3. **Double shrinkage** - When using master patterns, shrinkage compounds. Material waste in casting is significant (20-60% of metal poured becomes scrap/runners/risers).

4. **Casting yield** - Typical 40-80%. This directly impacts material cost. Our engine needs this when estimating casting costs.

5. **Pattern cost amortization** - Pattern costs (Rs 35,000-210,000 for tooling) amortized over batch size is a major factor in unit cost.

6. **Austempering advantage** - For defence applications, austempering gives 10x better impact energy at same hardness. Worth flagging in our recommendations.

7. **Indian sand sources** - Regional data (Rajkot, Hardwar, Orissa, WB, AP, Bihar) maps to our existing Indian regional cost framework.

8. **The book confirms** our existing material database is well-aligned with IS standards. The IS-to-AISI cross-reference table validates our material grade mappings.
